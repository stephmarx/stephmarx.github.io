Recursion

Sunday, January25, 2015
What is recursion?

Recursion is the process of defining something using itself. Recursion is esepcially useful in mathematics and (surprise, surprise) programming! A simple example of recursion could be the Fibonacci sequence, where a number is defined as the sum of the previous two numbers in the series. Recursion is somewhat similar to circular reasoning in logic (example: Blue is the color of the sky. The sky is the color blue), which in fact uses recursion to complete its "circle."

When is recursion used?

So, outside of representing fun and useful mathematical ideas, when would we use recursion? Recursion is also used in a variety of real-world engineering tasks, such as fly-by-wire systems for airplanes. The system receives an input about physical orientation and applies a calculation to the orientation, producing a corrected orientation.The system then passes this new orientation to itself and begins again. This prevents pilots from overshooting maneuvers and allows for a comfortable ride. This is similar to the psychophysical process our brains use to self-right using our inner ear. A signal is recursively processed and our position is corrected to keep us balanced and upright.

Recursion and programming

To be clear, a recursive process is very different from a loop. A loop simply refers back to an earlier step and begins again. A recursive process uses its result as an input. It defines its steps using its steps. Here's a simple pseudocode example:

        Step 1: Pick a number
        Step 2: Add 2 to the number
        Step 3: Multiply the number by 5
        Step 4: Pass result to step 2 and repeat.

Sometimes, a program can be made simpler by calling it recursively on itself until the desirable case (called the base case) has been reached. It is incredibly important that a base case be defined, so the recusive process stops, otherwise it will continue infinitely.
